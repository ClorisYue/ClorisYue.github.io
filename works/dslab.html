Digital Scholar Lab is a Gale tool for Digital Humanists & Scholars which offers solutions and possibilities to the most common challenges in the digital humanities. It has three key features - Create Content Sets, Analyze Documents, and Manage and Share.

Visualization is the important way to present document analysis results for digital humanists to understand documents and get insights hidden in the great amount of text. During the summer, I focus on designing interactive visualization of sentiment analysis and document clustering results.

2018 Summer UX Intern @ Gale, Cengage Learning | Team of 2 with one Senior UX Designer | Duration: June, 2018 - Aug, 2018 | Tools: Axure, Sketch
Status: Designed the interactive visualizations for two analysis methods. Under developing. A simplified version has been launched for test in the end of August 2018.

Design Challenge

What is Digital Humanities?

Digital Humanities is the field where scholars work in the intersection of Humanities and Computing by applying digital tools and methods on digital materials from the humanities field. It's still a growing discipline with great potential and exciting opportunities, as well as challenges to overcome. For example, how to find relevant data and content set, analyze materials with digital technologies, and collaborate.

Why visualization in Digital Scholar Lab?

With the developing of digital technologies, like text mining, it's much easier for researchers and scholars to analyze a great amount of data together and get the hidden value. Visualization is an efficient way to communicate some complex and abstract ideas, and can be useful for both research and pedagogue.

Digital Scholar Lab does not require any coding to help scholars who have no coding skills to analyze materials and visualize the results.

What is Sentiment Analysis?

This summer I focus on designing visualization for document clustering and sentiment analysis, here I'd like to share the design and process of sentiment analysis visualization as an example.

Sentiment analysis, also called opinion mining, aims to analyze and understand the attitude, the overall contextual polarity or the emotional reaction to the context of a speaker, writer...

There are a lot of ways to apply sentiment analysis. How can Digital Scholar Lab help to analyze materials at different levels and visualize results according to various user scenarios?

Design Overview

Key Features:

  • Text clean setting
  • Overall sentiment/ polarity levels analysis of a corpus
  • Sentiment/ polarity analysis at the document/ paragraph/ customized chunks level
  • Sentiment development analysis of sequenced documents

Persona

Design in the shoes of our users

For Digital Scholar Lab, six personae have been chosen. Ashley who is not that familiar with DH tools in general but aware of text analysis methods was been proven to be a major audience after the DSLab beta testing. So while designing the visualization tools, we should consider both users like Armando who have experience with collecting and analyzing data and major users like Ashley who are quite new to these analyzing tools.

User Scenario

Understanding the context and user paths

There are several user paths:

As Ashley, I expect:

To create a content set representing an event or topic within newspapers sequenced by time

  • From here I am able to discern sentiment at the article level
  • I can also discern the overall sentiment of my content set

To create a content set where I can discern sentiment at the paragraph level within each monograph.

  • I can discern a sentiment score at the paragraph level
  • I can also discern the overall sentiment of my content set

Final Design

Challenge 1

When user creates a content set of a topic or event, how can she/he discern the overall sentiment?

The visualization shows the overall sentiment distribution of the whole content set in five polarity levels, strongly negative, negative, neutral, positive, strongly positive. Ashley can click on the bar to view all documents within the content set that have the corresponding sentiment.

Iterations

Challenge 2

How can user know the sentiment change overtime of a topic or event by analyzing a set of sequential documents?

The line chart can most efficiently shows the sentiment change overtime. The top graph shows each document's polarity, and the bottom graph using the average polarity of each decades to show the mainstream sentiment change of that topic/events.

Iterations

Challenge 3

How can user discern the overall sentiment of the content set at the paragraph(or sentence, and other user defined chunks) level of each document?

The visualization shows the overall sentiment distribution and polarity value, key sentiment words and the sentiment change of each document in the whole corpus. User can click each card to view the detail of the document.

Iterations

Challenge 4

How can user discern the sentiment score at the paragraph(or sentence, and other user defined chunks) level of the document?

On the document/article page, adding a visualization summary panel under the existing DOC EXPLORER. The visualization contains three parts, the sentiment overlay, the overall sentiment of the document, and the sentiment development of the document. The sentiment overlay will highlight the document with corresponding colors by chunk. The overall sentiment shows the number of chunks of the polarity levels. The sentiment development has a zoom in view of the sentiment development of the current page and a scope controller showing the development of the whole document and a sliding window which can direct to the specific page.

Iterations

Challenge 5

How can user set and adjust text clean parameters for sentiment analysis?

Since the sentiment analysis is ran on the OCR text which might not be 100% accurate, before sentiment analysis there should be several text cleaning on the content and formatting to improve the credibility of analysis result. But for most primary resources, automatic OCR and text clean can still be hard to provide high quality resources. The manual and crowd-sourcing text cleaning is another major challenge under developing.

In the meanwhile, for pedagogical purpose, what's the purpose of each text clean rule should be explained to students like Ashley. And samples of how these rules work and affect the analysis should be provided.

Reflection

Designing and testing visualization is a little bit different. Since for people new to visualization they may not so sure about what's the most efficient way for them. So at early stage, visualization and DH experts can provide much more valuable feedbacks based on their experience. And when a relative complete design is created, the major users who are new to the tools should be involved into evaluation to test whether it's easier to learn and understand.

While designing visualization, I should balance the design and development. Based on the tech stack the development team is using and development timeline, some visualization might not be able to delivery, so it's important to consider all these in the beginning. Some design might be able to realized easily based on the current tech stack, some design might need extra investment. For example, the sentiment overlay is not a common visualization and need extra effort for the development team to realize. But after evaluations with users, DH specialists, product team, development team and other related teams, it was a feature worth extra investment.

If I had more time I'd like to do more user evaluations with DH students to mainly test whether text clean settings and visualization are understandable, how to better categories the visualization (group by analysis level or by context).

AVA

Time: Sep. 2017 - Now

Project for 2018 CHI student design competition

My Contributions:

Background & User Research, UX design, User Testing, Illustration (for the video) and Report

Other team members:

Akio Kakishima, Bingqing Shan, Qing Guo, Wenyang Mu

00. What's AVA?

AVA - Absentee Voting Assistant is a voting tool for the visually impaired and blind.
Key features:

- Work in conjunction with screen readers, such as VoiceOver;
- Help the visually impaired vote independently at home by operating the touch screen or voice control;
- Confirm the printed ballot by scanning.

01. Background

During the November 2016 Presidential election, 16 million people with disabilities reported voting. The turnout rate for people with visual impairment was 53.7%, which was much lower than people without disability, because they face barriers in voting. Specifically, they need assistance in filling out the ballot because the current system is not blind-friendly. Their voice is just as important as any other voter’s voice. Our solution aims to provide them this option.

02. User Research

After three user interviews, and field studies at three polling site in Ann Arbor, we found they value the right to voting independently, and prefer to vote at home and use voice commands over reading braille and write.

Target Users:

We focused on two groups: the completely blind user and the visually impaired individuals.

03. Ideation

We bascially brainstromed our solutions from three directions -- just a device, a template device with a corresponding App, and an App taking users and officers all together.

04. Sketches

We decided three criterias:
1. The solution needs to follow current laws and regulations, in another word, does not require political leveraging.
2. Blind people should not have to buy extra devices just for elections.
3. The solution should be as easy and secure as possible for the blind to vote independently.

One main solution to help the user “see” the ballot with three possible ways to fill out the ballots emerged:
1. using a physical template, like the signature guide.
2. locating and instructing the movement of user’s hands to fill out the check box by image processing and tracking algorithms in the app, and
3. using a printer to mark out the boxes on the original paper ballot.

05. User Testing

We conducted user testing on three blind individuals. The results showed that different users interpreted the instructions uniquely, so standardizing instructions become difficult. As a result, we decided to use a printer to mark out the boxes can be a better choice.

06. Work Flow

1. Receive the absentee ballot, launch the AVA app.
2. Input precinct information to pull up the correct ballot template.
3. Listen to the how-to-vote instructions.
4. Vote: The ballot will be read section by section, and user can select candidates and proposals.
5. Confirm: AVA will read the voting result to user to confirm or edit user’s choices.
6. Print: AVA will connect to the printer and ask the user to insert the paper ballot in correct direction.
7. Confirm printed result: User scans the printed ballot. AVA will recognize whether the correct candidate was filled in.
8. Seal and mail the ballot back.
